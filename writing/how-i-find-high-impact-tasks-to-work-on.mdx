# How I find high-impact tasks to work on

Many times throught out the years I worked on what I call _"side tasks"_, tasks that might be completelly unrelated to my team's scope or priorities, and that would likely never make it into any team's backlog or get picked up during a cool-down period.

**What makes something a side task**? It's usually something small enough that no product manager would write a spec for it, but impactful enough that many engineers silently suffer because of it. It could be a confusing API in a shared component, a repetitive manual step in a deployment process, or a cross-platform inconsistency that slows everyone down but belongs to no one. Nobody owns it. Nobody is coming to fix it. And precisely because of that — it stays broken, sometimes for years.

It's usually something that causes friction, here's some practical examples:
- Confusing APIs on UI Components, internal libraries or highly used abstractions
- Repetitive manual steps and boilerplates to setup things
- Architectural problems, that creates many other series of problems

a confusing API in a shared component, a repetitive manual step in a deployment process, a bad data structure that ripples across the codebase, or a deeper architectural problem that nobody has had the time or mandate to address. The size doesn't matter — a side task can be a one-hour fix or a weeks-long refactor. What defines it is that it belongs to no one. Nobody owns it, nobody is coming to fix it, and precisely because of that — it stays broken, sometimes for years.

## How to identify opportunities

The process for identifying these opportunities if improvements, which will later become side tasks you can work on is: **Look for moments of friction.**

Friction for me could have multiple forms:
- It could be an architectural problem.
- An onwership problem.
- A lack of process within a team or the company.
- A poorly designed API for a highly used module.
- Things that people often miss during a common process.

There are essentially two different moment I find those opportunities, during code reviews and while working on a regular task.

While working on a regular task that was assigned to me, I first go through and start analysing the modules where I need to introduce the changes to. While analysing and doing this initial exploration I might already see things that I don't like about that module or that could clearly be improved.

Is really hard to me to just add more functionality or extend an existing module if it contains problems that will affect this new functionality or that will get in the way of my task working the best it can.

A rule I try to follow is to always leave the module better than I first saw it, any small improvement compounds over time, you will also probably make mistakes and add things that might not be great, so is best to at least try to improve something there too.

On the majority of changes we might be making things more complex, harder to maintain or worse than it was before. Even though we might thing we are doing "the right thing" you might be wrong, so in my opinion we should at least attempt to improve something that is clearly incorrect or needs improvement.

Overtime those small details and improvements compounds and at the end you have a better software and products your users will use.

For code reviews, I try to look for common patterns, see if the engineer struggled to implement their feature or clearly missed something, if they did that's a point of attention to me. After I while reviewing code I start to notice common things that people miss while implementing a feature or any kind of changes they are introcuding.

Those are signs of friction to me as well, things we could do with that is creating custom ESLint rules to automatically catch those mistakes and keep the feedback loop faster.

We could also analyse the modules where those things are being missed and understand if there are different ways we could design the APIs of those modules to handle those things by defaulf or add guardrails around it.

We could add documentation to those parts of the codebase so that is clear to everyone what are the things they need to pay attention at while working there.

The pattern I see is to essentially slow down a bit in a few moments and really try to pay attention on what we are doing and what are the points of friction you notice, these are opportunities if you let them.

From those things, you might need to research deeply about a specific part of the codebase and come up with a great plan to improve things and resolve all the gaps that people usually miss, make the setup of thing simpler, improve the testing coverage of those modules and so on.

After that you will have more knowledge about that part of the codebase and once you make your proposals for improvements and merge those new changes, slowly people are going to start pointing others to you when the subject is related to those modules, you will start becoming a reference for those things.

This will also push you to start talking with other engineers from other teams of the company and increase your cross-domain impact, which is a key point for you to grow as an engineer across the organisation.

### The oppotunities list
As we are going through our day, we can't spend all our time doing improvements that noone asked us to do, we can pick it up a few that are thighly related to your tasks of couse.

But some of them are just too large to be picked it up right now and need more research, the path forward might not even be clear to you at this moment, but you definitely know that there is room to improve on this thing.

What I do in these moments is to take note on this item in my personal notes, just a bullet point with the name of the task or a short description of what you notice and what's in your mind about it.

Over time you will see that your list will contain multiple of those itens and you can start to see that some of them come back as you are working in different tasks, and those will get every time stronger in your list, because you can really feel the pain of those issues, and if you feel them, probably other engineers feel them too.

Same thing for code reviews, if you notice you are giving the same advice and type of suggestions to other engineers, add them to your list as well, these are also things you can thing on how to improve.

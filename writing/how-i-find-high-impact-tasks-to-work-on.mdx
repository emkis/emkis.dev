# How I Find Meaningful Work Nobody Assigned Me

Many times throughout the years I worked on what I call _"side tasks"_ — tasks that might be completely unrelated to my team's scope or priorities, and that would likely never make it into any team's backlog or get picked up during a cool-down period.

What makes something a side task? It's usually something that causes _friction_ — a confusing API in a shared component, a repetitive manual step in a deployment process, a bad data structure that ripples across the codebase, or a deeper architectural problem that nobody has had the time or mandate to address. The size doesn't matter — a side task can be a one-hour fix or a weeks-long refactor. What defines it is that it belongs to no one. Nobody owns it, nobody is coming to fix it, and precisely because of that — it stays broken, sometimes for years.

---

## Why work on them?

Based on this description, you might be thinking:

> "Are those side tasks relevant at all? If neither teams nor the company are prioritising them, why would you invest time on it?"

These are natural questions — and I know they're natural because Engineering Managers and Product Managers have asked me exactly that.

After working on many side tasks over the years, I can confidently say that completing them was one of the most effective things I did in my career to grow as an engineer, increase my visibility within a company, and build trust with other peers.

## What they look like

Friction can take multiple forms:

- A poorly designed API for a highly used module
- An architectural problem that creates a series of downstream issues
- An ownership problem where no team feels responsible
- A lack of process within a team or the company
- Things that people often miss during a common workflow

Here are a couple of practical examples of what I mean.

### Example 1: Analytics boilerplate

Let's say you're working on a task where you need to bind analytics events to existing user interactions in your product. You start doing research to see how other engineers do it in different products and teams, and you realise that setting up the events requires a lot of ceremony and boilerplate that is essentially the exact same everywhere. The API isn't great, requires a lot of repetitive setup, and things aren't ideal — but they work.

### Example 2: Reviewing code you don't own

Your team often receives pull requests to review from other teams where they are updating logic they own, but that exists within a module your team maintains. Your team becomes the bottleneck — even though you're not the owners of that piece of code, you need to review and approve it before it ships.

The friction here is: your team doesn't own that piece of code, you shouldn't be a required reviewer for it to ship. The cost is real — your team's time is spent reviewing things outside your scope, and the other team is blocked waiting on you.

## How to identify them

There are essentially two moments where I find these opportunities: **while working on a regular task** and **during code reviews**.

### While working on a task

When I pick up a task that was assigned to me, I first go through and start analysing the modules where I need to introduce changes. While doing this initial exploration, I might already see things I don't like about that module or that could clearly be improved.

It really bothers me to just add more code into a module that is not well organised or that clearly has problems. It's hard for me to just extend an existing module if it contains issues that will affect the new functionality or get in the way of my task working the best it can.

A rule I try to follow is to always leave the module better than I first saw it. Any small improvement compounds over time. You will also probably make mistakes and add things that might not be great, so it's best to at least try to improve something there too. Over time, those small details compound and at the end you have better software and products for your users.

### During code reviews

While reviewing code, I try to look for common patterns — whether the engineer struggled to implement their feature or clearly missed something. After reviewing enough code, I start to notice the same things people miss repeatedly while implementing a feature or introducing changes.

Those are signs of friction too. Things we could do about it:

- Create custom ESLint rules to automatically catch those mistakes and keep the feedback loop faster
- Analyse the modules where those things are being missed and understand if there are different ways to design the APIs to handle them by default or add guardrails
- Add documentation to those parts of the codebase so it's clear to everyone what they need to pay attention to

### The pattern

The pattern is to essentially slow down in a few key moments and really try to pay attention to what we are doing and what the points of friction are. These are opportunities, if you let them be.

## The hidden benefits
 g
After you do this research and start making improvements, something else starts to happen.

You'll have deeper knowledge about that part of the codebase than most people. Once you make your proposals and merge your changes, slowly people are going to start pointing others to you when the subject is related to those modules. You start becoming a reference.

This also pushes you to start talking with engineers from other teams across the company and increase your cross-domain impact, which is a key factor for growing as an engineer across the organisation.

## Where to start

From those things, you might need to research deeply a specific part of the codebase and come up with a plan to improve things — resolve the gaps that people usually miss, make the setup simpler, improve the testing coverage of those modules, and so on.

### Build your opportunities list

As we go through our day, we can't spend all our time doing improvements that no one asked us to do. We can pick up a few that are tightly related to our current tasks, of course. But some of them are just too large to tackle right now and need more research — the path forward might not even be clear yet. But you definitely know there is room to improve.

What I do in these moments is take a note — just a bullet point with the name of the task or a short description of what I noticed and what's in my mind about it.

Over time you'll see that your list contains multiple items, and some of them come back as you work on different tasks. Those get stronger in your list every time, because you can really feel the pain of those issues — and if you feel them, probably other engineers feel them too.

Same thing for code reviews. If you notice you're giving the same advice and suggestions to other engineers, add them to your list as well. These are things you can think about how to improve.
